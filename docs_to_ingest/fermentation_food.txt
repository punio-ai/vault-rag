Fermentation is one of the oldest methods of food preservation, dating back thousands of years. The process relies on microorganisms such as bacteria, yeast, and molds to convert sugars and starches into acids, gases, or alcohol. Common fermented foods include yogurt, sauerkraut, kimchi, miso, and sourdough bread, each with distinct microbial cultures that define their flavor and texture.

Beyond preservation, fermentation enhances the nutritional profile of foods by increasing bioavailability of vitamins and minerals. It also produces beneficial probiotic bacteria that support gut health. Recent research has explored using fermentation to reduce anti-nutritional compounds in legumes and grains, potentially improving food security in developing regions where these crops are dietary staples.
